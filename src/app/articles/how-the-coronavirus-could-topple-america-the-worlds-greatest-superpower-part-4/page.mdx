### Stock buy-backs: The worst thing to happen to the US economy.

Stock buy-backs are exactly what you think they are. The company buys back stock from the shareholders with cash (which could have come from debt). Why would they do that? Well there are 4 reasons but one is more important the other:

- To reward shareholders for investing in them. Awww that‚Äôs nice. ‚ù§Ô∏è
- To retain more voting control of the company.
- To buy back stock today so that they can sell it again in the future for more money. Maybe because they have some sweet new products in R&D.
- **Because many executives get bonuses based on stock performance.** Guess which one isn‚Äôt like the others? If you guessed #4, you are right! Because of the relaxed rules on executive compensation and stock buy-backs this created a perverse incentive for the company executives in charge of these decisions [to buy back as much stock as possible](https://money.cnn.com/2018/06/11/investing/stock-buybacks-sec-report/index.html). ü§î

> Remember who gets money from the Fed in the repo market?

Some of [the worst offenders of buy-backs have been the banks that received bailouts](https://www.bizjournals.com/charlotte/news/2019/06/28/fed-oks-bofas-plan-to-return-billions-of-dollars.html), and the airlines, [which spent 96% of free cashflows on buy-backs, while increasing luggage and seat fees](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2020-03-16/u-s-airlines-spent-96-of-free-cash-flow-on-buybacks-chart). They basically took public bail out money and/or cheap debt, ratcheted up consumer costs, and used their cash to buy stocks back, which rewarded shareholders and executives. Very few public US companies were immune to this behaviour. Even [Apple, in 1 quarter in 2018 alone, bought back $22.8 Billion dollars in stock](https://money.cnn.com/2018/06/11/investing/stock-buybacks-sec-report/index.html), more than any other company in a single quarter.

What this does is artificially pump the stock price, because there is a diminishing supply of stock out there and a consistent demand (the company buying). If you remember the basics of [supply and demand](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/l/law-of-supply-demand.asp), when this happens, the stock price goes up! ‚¨Üüí∞

Furthermore, if shareholders are being rewarded and prices are going up, the stocks look more enticing. ü§ë

Turns out a huge percentage of public companies in the USA have done this, because borrowing money has been dirt cheap for the last decade. As such, we‚Äôre now in a position where the US has had an artificially inflated stock market, pumped up job and wage growth, while companies are over leveraged with poor cash reserves. A recipe for a massive correction! üò≥

But this capital influx, accompanied by record low interest rates, has also produced another bad side effect‚Ä¶ too much debt.

### Public debt is at record levels

US public debt (ie. government debt they borrowed on behalf of tax payers), prior to COVID-19, was above 100% of GDP for the first time in the last 70 years, and only the second time post WWII. As you can see below, it has exploded since the 2008 financial crisis as [Quantitative Easing](https://www.thebalance.com/what-is-quantitative-easing-definition-and-explanation-3305881) has been in full force.
